# State Attorney General Multistate Litigation

**Summary:** State attorneys general have become major players in federal policy disputes through multistate litigation coalitions that challenge or defend executive-branch actions across ideologically aligned state groupings. Red-state and blue-state AG coalitions routinely file coordinated lawsuits challenging presidential policies—immigration enforcement, environmental regulation, healthcare mandates—with cases strategically filed in favorable forums. The Republican Attorneys General Association (RAGA) and Democratic Attorneys General Association (DAGA) formalize partisan coordination, and AG elections have become proxy battles for national policy agendas as the office's litigation power attracts significant campaign investment from national donors. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** State attorneys general form partisan coalitions to challenge federal policies through strategically filed multistate litigation, converting state-level offices into national policy instruments. [Observed]

### Actors and roles

- State attorneys general serve as chief legal officers for their states, wielding independent litigation authority to challenge or defend federal executive actions. [Observed]
- RAGA and DAGA coordinate partisan AG strategies, provide campaign funding, and facilitate multistate coalition formation for synchronized legal challenges. [Observed]
- Outside counsel retained by AG offices—often with ideological alignment—provide specialized litigation capacity and expand the reach of state legal challenges. [Observed]
- National advocacy organizations and donors invest in AG elections as a strategy for advancing litigation agendas through state-level legal officers. [Observed]
- Federal courts receive multistate challenges that present standing questions based on state sovereign interests, fiscal impacts, or parens patriae authority. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- Partisan AG associations identify federal policies vulnerable to legal challenge and recruit member AGs to join coordinated multistate complaints. [Observed]
- Lead AG offices draft complaints and coordinate legal strategy, dividing research, briefing, and argument responsibilities among coalition members. [Observed]
- Cases are filed in strategically selected jurisdictions, often exploiting forum-shopping opportunities to reach favorable judges and circuit courts. [Observed]
- Courts evaluate state standing claims—typically based on sovereign injury, economic harm, or regulatory burden—that differ from the standing requirements for private plaintiffs. [Observed]
- Successful challenges produce injunctions, settlements, or Supreme Court precedent that constrains or invalidates federal policy nationwide. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** Lead AGs in multistate coalitions control litigation strategy, settlement negotiations, and the allocation of resources, concentrating decision-making among a few offices. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** State standing doctrine creates bottlenecks that determine which challenges reach the merits—states' broader standing interests give AG coalitions access to courts that private plaintiffs often lack. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** AG election outcomes function as veto points, as newly elected AGs can withdraw from existing coalitions, settle pending cases, or reverse litigation positions. [Observed]

### Common failure modes

- Partisan coordination converts state legal officers into national political operatives, subordinating state-specific legal interests to national partisan strategy. [Observed]
- AG reliance on outside counsel creates potential conflicts when retained firms have interests that diverge from the states' legal obligations. [Observed]
- The proliferation of multistate challenges from opposing coalitions produces competing litigation that fragments federal policy and creates legal uncertainty. [Observed]
- National donor investment in AG races captures the office for policy agendas that may not align with in-state constituencies' priorities. [Observed]
