# Nationwide Injunction Power

**Summary:** Federal district judges have increasingly issued injunctions that block government policies across the entire nation, not just within their jurisdiction or for the parties before them. Nationwide injunctions—also called universal injunctions—allow a single district judge to halt executive action for all affected persons everywhere, creating enormous power concentration in individual trial judges and enabling forum-shopped litigation to produce immediate nationwide policy consequences. The practice has expanded dramatically since 2017, with judges appointed by both parties' presidents issuing sweeping orders, generating competing injunctions from different courts and forcing the Supreme Court to intervene through emergency stay applications. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** Nationwide injunctions allow single district judges to block government policies for all persons everywhere, concentrating enormous policy-halting power in trial courts. [Observed]

### Actors and roles

- Federal district judges issue nationwide injunctions under their equitable powers, determining the geographic and personal scope of relief without clear statutory limits. [Observed]
- The Department of Justice defends challenged policies and seeks stays or appeals of nationwide injunctions through circuit courts and emergency Supreme Court applications. [Observed]
- Plaintiffs' counsel—including state attorneys general and advocacy organizations—craft complaints and preliminary-injunction motions designed to secure the broadest possible relief from favorable judges. [Observed]
- The Supreme Court reviews emergency stay applications on the shadow docket, often resolving the practical effect of nationwide injunctions without full merits briefing. [Observed]
- Legal scholars debate whether the traditional equity practice supports universal relief or whether nationwide injunctions represent an unauthorized expansion of judicial power. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- Plaintiffs file challenges to executive actions in strategically selected courts, requesting preliminary injunctions with nationwide scope as part of their initial motions. [Observed]
- District judges evaluate likelihood of success, irreparable harm, balance of equities, and public interest under the preliminary-injunction standard (Winter v. NRDC framework). [Observed]
- Judges who find the standard met issue orders specifying geographic and personal scope, increasingly choosing nationwide scope to provide "complete relief" to plaintiff classes. [Observed]
- The government seeks emergency stays from circuit courts and, if denied, from the Supreme Court, creating a parallel emergency-docket litigation track. [Observed]
- Competing nationwide injunctions from different courts—one blocking and one permitting the same policy—create legal chaos requiring appellate or Supreme Court resolution. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** Individual district judges become policy gatekeepers when they issue orders with nationwide scope, exercising power traditionally associated with legislatures or the Supreme Court. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** The preliminary-injunction standard creates a low-threshold bottleneck where policies can be halted nationwide based on a likelihood-of-success assessment rather than final merits adjudication. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** Emergency-stay applications to the Supreme Court create a veto point where the Court can override or sustain district-court injunctions without full briefing or oral argument. [Observed]

### Common failure modes

- Nationwide injunctions halt policies affecting millions based on a single judge's preliminary assessment, before full adversarial briefing and evidentiary development. [Observed]
- Competing injunctions from different courts create conflicting legal regimes, undermining legal certainty and forcing resolution through emergency procedures rather than deliberate appellate review. [Observed]
- The availability of nationwide relief reduces incentives for litigants to build coalitions, pursue legislative solutions, or develop test cases through gradual litigation. [Observed]
- Emergency-stay practice on the Supreme Court's shadow docket produces unsigned, unreasoned orders with enormous policy consequences but minimal precedential value. [Observed]
