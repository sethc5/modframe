# State Supreme Court Selection and Retention

**Summary:** State supreme court justices are selected through a patchwork of methods—partisan election, nonpartisan election, merit selection (Missouri Plan), gubernatorial appointment, and legislative appointment—that produce dramatically different relationships between judicial independence, democratic accountability, and interest-group influence. Judicial elections have become increasingly expensive and nationalized, with outside spending by advocacy groups, trial-lawyer associations, and business coalitions transforming retention elections into proxy battles over tort reform, criminal justice, and regulatory policy. The selection method fundamentally shapes court behavior: elected judges face documented pressure to rule in favor of contributors and against criminal defendants as elections approach. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** State supreme court selection methods—elections, merit commissions, or appointments—determine the balance between judicial independence and external political pressure on court decisions. [Observed]

### Actors and roles

- State supreme court justices exercise final interpretive authority over state constitutions and statutes, making selection method a critical determinant of judicial behavior. [Observed]
- Judicial nominating commissions (in merit-selection states) screen candidates and present shortlists to governors, controlling access to the bench through professional gatekeeping. [Observed]
- Outside spending groups—including the Judicial Crisis Network, trial-lawyer PACs, and business coalitions—invest heavily in contested judicial elections to influence court composition. [Observed]
- State bar associations participate in candidate evaluation, merit-commission membership, and public education about judicial qualifications and performance. [Observed]
- Governors appoint justices in appointment states and select from commission shortlists in merit-selection states, exercising significant influence over court composition. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- Vacancies arise through retirement, death, term expiration, or electoral defeat; the applicable selection method determines the replacement process. [Observed]
- In election states, candidates campaign for judicial office, raising funds, making public appearances, and seeking endorsements while navigating judicial-conduct constraints on political speech. [Observed]
- In merit-selection states, nominating commissions solicit applications, interview candidates, and forward shortlists (typically three names) to the governor for selection. [Observed]
- Retention elections in merit-selection states require sitting justices to secure majority voter approval, creating periodic vulnerability to organized opposition campaigns. [Observed]
- Justices who assume office through any method serve terms ranging from six years to life tenure, with reselection processes creating ongoing political exposure. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** Nominating commissions in merit-selection states exercise decisive power over who reaches the bench, with commission composition determining ideological balance. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** Campaign fundraising requirements in election states create dependence on contributors who subsequently appear as litigants, generating structural conflicts of interest. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** Organized retention-election opposition campaigns can unseat individual justices, creating chilling effects on judicial independence across the entire court. [Observed]

### Common failure modes

- Judicial campaign contributions from litigants and their attorneys create appearance-of-corruption problems that undermine public confidence in court impartiality. [Observed]
- Low-information judicial elections produce outcomes driven by name recognition and party labels rather than qualifications or judicial philosophy. [Observed]
- Merit-commission capture by bar associations or political factions can produce shortlists that reflect narrow professional elite preferences rather than diverse judicial perspectives. [Observed]
- Retention-election attacks targeting specific unpopular decisions create incentives for judges to avoid politically controversial rulings regardless of legal merit. [Observed]

- Compare judicial decision patterns across selection methods to determine whether elected judges behave differently from appointed judges on politically salient issues. [Observed]
