# State Medicaid Administration and Federal Waivers

**Summary:** Medicaid—the joint federal-state health insurance program covering over 90 million Americans—operates through state-administered plans that vary enormously in eligibility criteria, benefit packages, provider-reimbursement rates, and managed-care structures. States exercise substantial policy discretion through plan amendments and Section 1115 waivers that allow experimentation outside standard program rules, creating a laboratory where different approaches to public health coverage coexist across state lines. The Affordable Care Act's Medicaid expansion—accepted by 40 states as of 2025 but rejected by 10—produced the most visible example of state discretion determining whether millions of low-income adults receive health coverage. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** States administer Medicaid with substantial discretion over eligibility, benefits, and delivery systems, using federal waivers to experiment with coverage models that vary dramatically across state lines. [Observed]

### Actors and roles

- State Medicaid agencies design and administer coverage programs, set provider-reimbursement rates, and manage enrollment processes within federal guidelines. [Observed]
- State governors and legislatures make expansion and waiver decisions that determine eligibility levels and program design for their populations. [Observed]
- The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) approves state plan amendments and Section 1115 waivers, exercising federal oversight over state experimentation. [Observed]
- Managed-care organizations contract with state Medicaid programs to deliver services, creating a private-sector intermediary layer in benefit administration. [Observed]
- Healthcare providers and hospital associations advocate for reimbursement rates that sustain services while navigating the complexities of state-specific Medicaid rules. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- State legislatures establish the legal and fiscal framework for Medicaid, including eligibility levels, optional benefits, and funding appropriations. [Observed]
- State Medicaid agencies implement program design through enrollee communications, provider-network management, and claims-processing systems. [Observed]
- States seeking to deviate from standard Medicaid rules submit Section 1115 waiver applications to CMS, proposing experimental program features for federal approval. [Observed]
- CMS reviews waiver applications, negotiates terms and conditions, and approves or denies proposals based on budget neutrality and program objectives. [Observed]
- Approved waivers operate under time-limited authority with monitoring and evaluation requirements, though renewal has historically been routine. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** Governors decide whether to pursue Medicaid expansion or waiver experiments, with expansion decisions alone determining coverage for hundreds of thousands of residents. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** CMS waiver-approval authority creates a federal chokepoint where administration priorities influence which state experiments advance. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** State legislative appropriations control determines Medicaid funding adequacy, with budget pressures creating incentives to restrict eligibility or benefits. [Observed]

### Common failure modes

- State refusal to expand Medicaid creates coverage gaps where adults in non-expansion states earn too much for traditional Medicaid but too little for marketplace subsidies. [Observed]
- Low provider-reimbursement rates reduce physician participation, creating access barriers for enrollees who have nominal coverage but cannot find providers. [Observed]
- Waiver conditions (work requirements, premiums, lockout periods) create administrative barriers that reduce enrollment among eligible populations. [Observed]
- Managed-care profit incentives may conflict with comprehensive care delivery, producing access and quality problems masked by enrollment statistics. [Observed]

### What evidence would prove/disprove key claims

- Compare health outcomes, uninsured rates, and hospital financial stability between expansion and non-expansion states to assess the effects of coverage decisions. [Observed]
- Evaluate Section 1115 waiver experiments using the required evaluation designs to determine whether work requirements, premiums, and other conditions improve or harm coverage outcomes. [Observed]
- Track provider participation rates across states with different reimbursement levels to assess the relationship between payment adequacy and beneficiary access. [Observed]
- Analyze managed-care performance metrics (access, quality, cost) across states to evaluate whether privatized delivery improves Medicaid outcomes. [Hypothesis]
