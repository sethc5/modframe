# State Legislative Redistricting and Gerrymandering

**Summary:** State legislative redistricting—the decennial process of redrawing district boundaries for state legislatures and US House seats—concentrates enormous political power in whichever institution controls map-drawing, typically the state legislature itself. Partisan gerrymandering uses sophisticated mapping software and granular voter data to draw districts that maximize one party's seat share while minimizing competitive elections, producing legislatures whose partisan composition diverges significantly from statewide vote shares. The Supreme Court's decision in Rucho v Common Cause (2019) declared federal courts unable to adjudicate partisan gerrymandering claims, shifting the battleground to state courts, independent commissions, and ballot initiatives. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** Redistricting concentrates political power in map-drawing authorities that use data-driven gerrymandering to predetermine electoral outcomes for a decade. [Observed]

### Actors and roles

- State legislatures draw redistricting maps in most states, with the majority party controlling boundary decisions that determine electoral competition for the following decade. [Observed]
- Independent redistricting commissions (in approximately 10 states) draw maps with varying degrees of insulation from legislative control and partisan influence. [Observed]
- Political data firms and mapping consultants provide the voter-file data, demographic analysis, and geographic-information software that enable precision gerrymandering. [Observed]
- State courts adjudicate redistricting challenges under state constitutional provisions that may provide stronger anti-gerrymandering protections than the federal Constitution. [Observed]
- Voting-rights organizations and fair-maps advocacy groups challenge gerrymandered maps through litigation, ballot initiatives, and public education campaigns. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- Census data is released, providing the population counts that trigger redistricting requirements under state and federal law. [Observed]
- The designated map-drawing authority (legislature, commission, or hybrid body) develops proposed district boundaries using mapping software and demographic data. [Observed]
- Public hearings provide opportunities for citizen input, though the degree to which input influences final maps varies dramatically across states. [Observed]
- Final maps are adopted through legislative vote, commission approval, or other state-specific mechanisms, often along party-line divisions. [Observed]
- Litigation challenges test maps against Voting Rights Act requirements, state constitutional provisions, and (in some states) explicit anti-gerrymandering criteria. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** The majority party in the state legislature controls map-drawing in most states, exercising unilateral authority over electoral district design. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** Access to sophisticated mapping software and voter-file data creates technical barriers that advantage well-resourced partisan operations over public-interest alternatives. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** Gubernatorial veto of legislative maps provides a check in some states, but trifecta control eliminates this constraint. [Observed]

### Common failure modes

- Partisan gerrymandering produces legislatures whose partisan composition does not reflect statewide voter preferences, undermining representational legitimacy. [Observed]
- Racial gerrymandering—whether packing minority voters into few districts or cracking them across many—dilutes minority voting power in violation of the Voting Rights Act. [Observed]
- Incumbent-protection gerrymandering reduces electoral competition, insulating legislators from accountability and reducing responsiveness to constituent preferences. [Observed]
- Commission-based reforms may be captured by partisan actors who dominate the appointment process for ostensibly independent commissioners. [Observed]

### What evidence would prove/disprove key claims

- Compare efficiency-gap and partisan-bias metrics across states with legislative versus commission-based redistricting to measure the gerrymandering effect. [Observed]
- Track electoral competitiveness (margin of victory distributions) before and after redistricting cycles to assess the impact on democratic competition. [Observed]
- Analyze the relationship between map-drawer partisanship and subsequent legislative seat shares relative to statewide vote shares. [Observed]
- Evaluate commission-drawn maps against the same metrics to determine whether independent redistricting meaningfully reduces partisan bias. [Observed]
