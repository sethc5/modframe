# Interstate Compacts and Multistate Cooperation

**Summary:** Interstate compacts—formal agreements between states authorized by the Constitution's Compact Clause—create binding governance structures that operate outside normal federal and state legislative processes, managing shared resources, coordinating regulatory policy, and resolving cross-border disputes. Over 200 active compacts govern issues from water allocation (Colorado River Compact) to professional licensing (Nurse Licensure Compact) to law enforcement (Interstate Compact for Adult Offender Supervision), creating a shadow layer of governance that most citizens are unaware of. Compacts concentrate authority in interstate commissions whose members are appointed rather than elected, producing governance structures with substantial power but limited democratic accountability. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** Interstate compacts create binding multistate governance structures that manage shared resources and coordinate policy outside normal democratic accountability frameworks. [Observed]

### Actors and roles

- State governors and legislatures negotiate and ratify interstate compacts, committing their states to binding agreements that may constrain future policy choices. [Observed]
- Interstate compact commissions administer agreement terms, resolve disputes, and make policy decisions on behalf of member states. [Observed]
- Congress exercises consent authority over compacts that affect federal interests, though many compacts operate without explicit congressional approval. [Observed]
- Federal courts adjudicate compact disputes, interpreting agreement terms and resolving conflicts between member states. [Observed]
- Stakeholder organizations and affected industries participate in compact governance through advisory committees, public comment, and litigation. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- States identify a shared governance challenge that requires multistate coordination beyond what individual state action can achieve. [Observed]
- Negotiators from participating states draft compact language specifying governance structures, member obligations, enforcement mechanisms, and withdrawal procedures. [Observed]
- State legislatures ratify the compact through identical or substantially similar enactments, with congressional consent sought when required. [Observed]
- The compact commission organizes, appoints staff, establishes rules, and begins administering the agreement's substantive provisions. [Observed]
- Ongoing governance operates through commission meetings, rulemaking, dispute resolution, and periodic compact review or amendment. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** Compact commissioners (typically gubernatorial appointees) make binding policy decisions affecting multiple states with limited public visibility or electoral accountability. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** Compact amendments typically require unanimous consent of all member states, creating veto power for any single member over governance changes. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** Federal consent requirements give Congress leverage over compacts that affect federal interests, potentially blocking otherwise agreed-upon interstate arrangements. [Observed]

### Common failure modes

- Democratic accountability deficits arise when compact commissioners make significant policy decisions without direct electoral oversight or transparent deliberation. [Observed]
- Compact rigidity prevents adaptation when circumstances change, as amendment requires multistate agreement that is difficult to achieve. [Observed]
- Unequal bargaining power among member states may produce compact terms that favor larger or wealthier states at the expense of smaller participants. [Observed]
- Withdrawal provisions create instability when states exit compacts in response to unfavorable decisions, undermining cooperative frameworks. [Observed]
