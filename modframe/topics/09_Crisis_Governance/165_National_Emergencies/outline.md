# National Emergencies

**Summary:** The National Emergencies Act of 1976 (NEA) was designed to constrain presidential emergency power by requiring formal declarations, congressional reporting, and periodic renewal — but in practice it has become a mechanism for accumulating and indefinitely extending executive authority with minimal congressional oversight. [Observed] When a president declares a national emergency under the NEA, it activates access to over 130 statutory emergency powers scattered across federal law, granting authority over military deployment, economic sanctions, communications regulation, government contracting, and more. [Observed — source: Brennan Center, *A Guide to Emergency Powers*] As of 2024, over 40 national emergencies remain active, some dating back to the Carter administration. [Observed] Congress has never successfully used the NEA's termination mechanism (requiring a joint resolution, which the president can veto) to end a declared emergency, making the NEA's oversight provisions structurally inoperative. [Observed] The result is a permanent state of declared emergency in which presidents accumulate emergency authorities without meaningful temporal or substantive constraints. [Inferred]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** The National Emergencies Act grants presidents access to 130+ emergency powers through formal declarations that Congress has never terminated, creating a permanent state of accumulated emergency authority. [Inferred]

### Actors and roles

- **The President** — declares national emergencies, activating statutory emergency powers; has sole discretion over the scope and duration of declarations. [Observed] Incentive: access to expanded executive authority without congressional legislation. Constraint: must specify the statutory provisions activated (but the requirement is procedural, not substantive). [Observed]
- **Congress** — designed as the check on emergency power through the NEA's termination mechanism (joint resolution), but the veto-proof supermajority requirement makes termination structurally impossible without presidential cooperation. [Observed]
- **Office of Legal Counsel (OLC)** — provides legal opinions on the scope of emergency powers; interpretations tend to favor expansive presidential authority. [Observed]
- **Federal agencies** — implement emergency authorities once activated; agency heads gain expanded discretion under emergency conditions. [Observed]
- **Courts** — review challenges to emergency declarations but have generally deferred to executive branch on the threshold question of whether an emergency exists. [Observed]
- **Brennan Center for Justice** — primary nongovernmental tracker of emergency declarations and emergency powers. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- The president identifies a situation (foreign, domestic, or economic) that the administration characterizes as requiring emergency response. [Observed]
- The president issues a proclamation declaring a national emergency under the NEA (50 U.S.C. §§ 1601–1651), specifying which statutory emergency powers the declaration activates. [Observed]
- The declaration is transmitted to Congress and published in the Federal Register. [Observed]
- Activated statutory powers take immediate effect, granting the executive branch expanded authority in the specified areas. [Observed]
- The emergency continues indefinitely unless the president terminates it or Congress passes a joint resolution of termination (which the president can veto). [Observed]
- Each September, the president is required to publish a notice of continuation for each active emergency; failure to publish automatically terminates the emergency. [Observed]
- Congress is required to consider the emergency every six months, but this requirement has been routinely ignored since the NEA's enactment. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** The president has unilateral authority to declare emergencies and determine which powers are activated; no prior congressional authorization is required. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** The NEA termination mechanism (joint resolution subject to presidential veto) makes congressional termination effectively impossible without a two-thirds supermajority in both chambers. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** Courts can review the legality of specific actions taken under emergency authority, but generally do not second-guess the underlying emergency declaration itself. [Observed]

### Common failure modes

- The NEA's six-month congressional review requirement has been consistently ignored since 1976; Congress has never conducted the mandated periodic reviews. [Observed]
- National emergencies are declared for situations that are chronic rather than acute (e.g., sanctions regimes), creating permanent emergency authorities for conditions that have no expected resolution date. [Observed]
- The accumulation of 40+ active emergencies normalizes the state of emergency, making it politically and institutionally unremarkable — reducing the urgency of congressional oversight. [Observed]
- Presidents use emergency declarations to bypass congressional opposition on policy priorities (e.g., border wall funding), stretching the concept of "emergency" beyond its original intended scope. [Observed]
- The 130+ scattered statutory emergency powers have never been comprehensively cataloged by the executive branch, and few members of Congress are aware of what powers an emergency declaration activates. [Observed]
