# State-Level Lobbying and Preemption Campaigns

**Summary:** State legislatures are targeted by industry lobbying campaigns that seek to establish favorable regulatory frameworks, block local regulation through preemption laws, and create model-legislation pipelines that can be replicated across multiple states simultaneously. Organizations like the American Legislative Exchange Council provide turn-key legislative drafts, lobbying coordination, and legislator relationship-building that amplify corporate influence at the state level where disclosure rules, media coverage, and countervailing advocacy capacity are often weaker than at the federal level. This matters because state-level regulatory decisions increasingly determine the operative legal environment for industries seeking to avoid federal regulation or preempt local innovation. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** State-level lobbying campaigns exploit weaker disclosure environments and model-legislation networks to establish industry-favorable regulatory frameworks and preempt local regulation at scale. [Observed]

### Actors and roles

- Industry lobbyists and corporate government affairs teams engage state legislators through direct contacts, campaign contributions, and participation in model-legislation organizations. [Observed]
- Model-legislation organizations (ALEC, State Policy Network, Council of State Governments) provide drafted bill language, policy briefs, and legislator convening events funded by corporate members. [Observed]
- State legislators, often operating with minimal staff and research capacity, rely on outside-provided legislative language and policy analysis that may reflect industry preferences. [Observed]
- Local governments targeted by preemption efforts face overrides of their regulatory authority without proportional representation in state-level lobbying dynamics. [Observed]
- Public-interest advocates and state-level policy organizations provide countervailing research and mobilization but typically operate with substantially fewer resources than industry lobbying campaigns. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- An industry identifies a regulatory threat—typically emerging local regulation or proposed state legislation—and develops a preemption or model-legislation strategy. [Observed]
- Model-legislation organizations draft bill language, recruit legislative sponsors, and provide supporting materials including talking points, fiscal analyses, and constitutional arguments. [Observed]
- State lobbyists build legislative coalitions through direct advocacy, trade association coordination, campaign contribution strategy, and grassroots mobilization of industry stakeholders. [Observed]
- The legislature considers the measure, often with limited public deliberation, committee staff analysis, or media scrutiny compared to federal legislative processes. [Observed]
- Successful preemption laws override local regulatory authority, and model-legislation templates are replicated across multiple state legislatures, creating parallel policy adoption patterns. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** State legislative leadership and committee chairs control scheduling and bill advancement, and their relationships with industry lobbyists often develop in low-visibility environments with limited press coverage. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** Limited state legislative staff and research capacity creates information dependency on lobbyist-provided analysis, making industry-supplied materials disproportionately influential. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** Governor vetoes provide a check on industry-backed legislation, but industry campaign contributions and lobbying extend to the executive, reducing veto probability on industry-priority measures. [Inferred]

### Common failure modes

- Weak state-level disclosure rules allow lobbying expenditures and relationships to operate with less transparency than comparable federal activity. [Observed]
- Model-legislation campaigns can produce state laws that override locally appropriate regulation without adequate analysis of local conditions or impact. [Observed]
- The resource asymmetry between industry lobbying budgets and public-interest advocacy capacity is magnified at the state level, where countervailing organizations are often volunteer-run or understaffed. [Observed]
- Simultaneous multi-state campaigns can saturate public-interest opposition capacity, making effective resistance difficult across all targeted jurisdictions simultaneously. [Inferred]

### What evidence would prove/disprove key claims

- Track model-legislation adoption rates across states to measure the scale and speed of coordinated industry campaigns. [Observed]
- Compare state lobbying disclosure requirements and compliance rates across jurisdictions to assess the transparency environment for state-level influence. [Observed]
- Analyze preemption law impacts on local regulatory capacity to test whether preemption produces regulatory gaps or achieves industry-claimed uniformity benefits. [Inferred]
- Map industry lobbying expenditures at state versus federal levels to measure the allocation of influence resources across governance tiers. [Hypothesis]
