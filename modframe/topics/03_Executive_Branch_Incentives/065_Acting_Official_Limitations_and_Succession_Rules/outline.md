# Acting Official Limitations and Succession Rules

**Summary:** Acting official limitations and succession rules govern who may temporarily fill vacant senior executive branch positions and for how long, building on and extending the framework established in the Federal Vacancies Reform Act. This matters because succession planning determines continuity of agency operations when Senate-confirmed positions are vacant, and rule design affects whether acting officials function as independent leaders or as White House-directed placeholders who avoid Senate scrutiny. Agency-specific succession statutes, executive orders designating orders of succession, and Vacancies Act provisions create overlapping authority that can be strategically navigated to install preferred acting officials. The interaction between these frameworks creates ambiguity about legal authority, accountability, and time limits. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** Overlapping succession frameworks — FVRA, agency-specific statutes, and executive orders — create strategic flexibility for filling senior vacancies with acting officials who bypass Senate confirmation. [Observed]

### Actors and roles

- The president designates acting officials under FVRA or directs agency heads to establish succession orders through executive order, creating multiple pathways to install preferred temporary leaders. [Observed]
- Agency heads implement succession plans within their departments, designating first assistants and alternative succession orders for key positions. [Observed]
- The Government Accountability Office monitors FVRA compliance, tracking time-limit violations and actions taken by potentially unauthorized acting officials. [Observed]
- The Senate monitors vacancies and acting appointments, using confirmation hearings and holds to assert its advise-and-consent authority, though enforcement of time limits is primarily political. [Observed]
- Courts increasingly adjudicate challenges to agency actions taken by acting officials whose appointment or tenure may violate FVRA requirements. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- A Senate-confirmed position becomes vacant through resignation, termination, or other departure, triggering succession and vacancy provisions. [Observed]
- The FVRA provides three categories of eligible acting officials: the first assistant, senior agency employees at GS-15 or above, and individuals already confirmed by the Senate to another position. [Observed]
- The president or agency head selects an acting official from eligible categories; alternatively, an agency-specific succession statute may provide different eligibility rules that supplement or override FVRA. [Observed]
- FVRA time limits (generally 210 days, with extensions during nomination periods) constrain acting service, but strategic nomination and withdrawal cycles can reset or extend these periods. [Observed]
- If time limits expire without a confirmed successor, the acting official must stop performing exclusive functions of the office, though agencies may argue that certain actions fall outside this restriction. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** The president controls which eligible individual serves as acting official and can strategically select from multiple FVRA categories or invoke agency-specific statutes to install preferred candidates. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** Senate confirmation delays create extended vacancy periods that normalize acting official governance, reducing political pressure to submit and confirm permanent nominees. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** GAO compliance monitoring and judicial review of actions by potentially unauthorized acting officials provide ex post checks, but these operate slowly relative to the pace of agency decision-making. [Observed]

### Common failure modes

- Strategic non-nomination allows indefinite acting service by avoiding the FVRA clock triggers associated with formal nomination, keeping preferred officials in position without Senate scrutiny. [Observed]
- Overlapping succession authorities create ambiguity about which framework governs a specific vacancy, enabling creative legal interpretations that expand presidential appointment flexibility. [Observed]
- Time-limit violations produce validity questions about agency actions taken after FVRA periods expire, creating legal uncertainty for regulated parties and agency programs. [Observed]
- Acting officials may lack institutional knowledge, stakeholder relationships, and political independence compared to confirmed nominees, reducing agency operational effectiveness. [Inferred]

### What evidence would prove/disprove key claims

- Track vacancy durations, acting official tenures, and nomination timelines across administrations to identify patterns of strategic delay and FVRA clock manipulation. [Observed]
- Analyze GAO FVRA compliance reports to measure violation rates and identify agencies or positions with systemic non-compliance. [Observed]
- Compare agency output metrics (rulemaking pace, enforcement actions, program administration) during acting-official periods versus confirmed-official periods to test operational effectiveness claims. [Inferred]
- Evaluate whether mandatory nomination deadlines, automatic succession triggers, or enhanced GAO enforcement authority would reduce strategic vacancy maintenance. [Hypothesis]
