# Interagency Process Dynamics

**Summary:** The interagency process is the formal system through which executive branch departments and agencies coordinate policy across jurisdictional boundaries, primarily through the National Security Council system's hierarchy of committees, deputies committees, and principals committees. This matters because most major policy decisions require input from multiple agencies with competing missions, budgets, and institutional cultures, and the interagency process determines which perspectives reach the president and how implementation directives are distributed. The process concentrates coordinating authority in the White House National Security Advisor and NSC staff, who control meeting agendas, participant lists, and paper flow, giving them outsized influence over policy outcomes relative to their formal authority. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** The interagency process coordinates executive branch policy through a hierarchical committee system controlled by NSC staff, concentrating agenda-setting and information-filtering power in the White House. [Observed]

### Actors and roles

- The National Security Advisor chairs the principals committee, controls NSC staff operations, and manages information flow to the president on national security and foreign policy matters. [Observed]
- NSC staff members draft policy options, prepare meeting agendas, circulate papers, and write summary-of-conclusions documents that become implementation directives. [Observed]
- Cabinet secretaries and agency heads participate in principals committee meetings, representing departmental equities and implementation capacity in policy deliberations. [Observed]
- Deputies from each agency participate in the deputies committee, which filters issues, resolves lower-level disagreements, and prepares decision packages for principals. [Observed]
- Interagency policy committees at the assistant-secretary level conduct detailed analysis, develop options, and elevate unresolved issues through the committee hierarchy. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- A policy issue is identified and assigned to an interagency policy committee, which convenes representatives from relevant departments and agencies. [Observed]
- The IPC develops options, conducts analysis, and attempts to resolve disagreements; unresolved issues are elevated to the deputies committee. [Observed]
- The deputies committee reviews elevated issues, applies higher-level political judgment, and either resolves disputes or frames decisions for principals. [Observed]
- The principals committee considers major policy decisions, with the National Security Advisor framing options and managing deliberation flow. [Observed]
- Summary-of-conclusions documents record decisions and assign implementation responsibilities to specific agencies, with NSC staff monitoring execution compliance. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** NSC staff control meeting agendas, participant lists, and paper circulation, determining which issues and perspectives reach senior decision-makers. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** Deputies committee serves as the primary filter for issues reaching principals, with deputy-level officials controlling elevation decisions and framing. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** Agencies can resist implementation through slow-rolling, reinterpretation of directives, or bureaucratic non-compliance, creating de facto vetoes over policies they oppose. [Inferred]

### Common failure modes

- NSC staff expansion beyond coordination into operational roles duplicates agency functions and creates accountability gaps when operations bypass departmental chains of command. [Observed]
- Agenda control by NSC staff allows issue suppression or framing bias that systematically advantages certain policy perspectives over others. [Inferred]
- Summary-of-conclusions documents may not accurately reflect deliberation outcomes, creating disputes over what was actually decided and authorized. [Observed]
- Agency non-participation or inadequate representation at IPC meetings produces policies that lack implementation feasibility or miss critical departmental constraints. [Observed]

### What evidence would prove/disprove key claims

- Track NSC staff size, organizational structure, and operational involvement across administrations to measure coordination-to-operations drift. [Observed]
- Compare agency input at IPC stage with final principals committee decisions to test whether the filtering process systematically excludes certain perspectives. [Inferred]
- Analyze implementation compliance rates for interagency directives to measure agency responsiveness and identify slow-rolling patterns. [Inferred]
- Evaluate whether alternative coordination models (e.g., lead-agency approaches) produce different policy outcomes or implementation rates. [Hypothesis]
