# Ethics Waivers for Political Appointees

**Summary:** Federal conflict-of-interest law (18 U.S.C. § 208) prohibits government officials from participating in matters affecting their personal financial interests, but authorizes agency heads to grant waivers when the financial interest is deemed "not so substantial as to be deemed likely to affect the integrity" of the official's services. [Observed] These waivers — along with ethics pledges issued by executive order at the start of each administration — create a parallel system where conflicts of interest are not eliminated but formally authorized, converting structural conflicts into documented permissions. [Inferred] Waiver disclosure practices vary dramatically across agencies and administrations: some waivers are published proactively, others are released only under FOIA, and the criteria for granting waivers are applied with broad discretion that resists meaningful external review. [Observed] The structural effect is an ethics system where the existence of a waiver mechanism provides a procedural path for appointees to retain financial interests that the underlying statute was designed to prevent. [Inferred]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** Ethics waivers allow agency heads to authorize appointees to participate in matters affecting their financial interests, converting the conflict-of-interest prohibition from a hard constraint into a discretionary permission. [Inferred]

### Actors and roles

- **Agency heads** — exercise statutory authority to grant ethics waivers under 18 U.S.C. § 208(b)(1) and (b)(3), determining that the financial interest at issue is not substantial enough to affect the appointee's integrity. [Observed] Incentive: retain qualified appointees who may bring industry expertise but also industry entanglements. Constraint: OGE can review waivers but cannot veto them; public disclosure creates reputational risk. [Observed]
- **Political appointees (waiver recipients)** — request or receive waivers to participate in matters involving former employers, clients, or financial interests. [Observed] Incentive: participate fully in their appointed role without divesting or recusing. Constraint: waiver terms may impose conditions (time limits, matter-specific restrictions) that constrain participation scope. [Observed]
- **Office of Government Ethics (OGE)** — reviews waiver grants, maintains waiver databases for executive branch, and issues guidance on waiver criteria. [Observed — source: 5 C.F.R. § 2640] Constraint: OGE can comment on waivers but cannot block or revoke them; its review authority is advisory. [Observed]
- **Designated Agency Ethics Officials (DAEOs)** — advise agency heads on waiver requests, draft waiver documents, and track compliance with waiver conditions. [Observed]
- **White House Counsel** — administers ethics pledge waivers under the executive order ethics pledge issued at the start of each administration. [Observed] Incentive: allow appointees to function effectively while maintaining the appearance of ethics compliance. [Inferred]
- **Congressional oversight committees** — can request waiver disclosures and hold hearings on waiver practices; have limited enforcement authority over executive branch waiver decisions. [Observed]
- **Transparency organizations** (CREW, POGO) — file FOIA requests for waivers, analyze waiver patterns, and publicize potential conflicts of interest. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- A political appointee is confirmed or appointed to a position where their prior employment, financial holdings, or client relationships create potential conflicts under 18 U.S.C. § 208(a). [Observed]
- The appointee's DAEO evaluates the conflict and recommends resolution: divestiture, recusal, resignation from conflicting positions, or waiver. [Observed]
- If a waiver is recommended, the DAEO drafts a waiver document specifying the financial interest, the matters at issue, and the basis for determining the interest is not substantial. [Observed]
- The agency head (or White House Counsel for ethics pledge waivers) signs the waiver, which may include conditions such as matter-specific limitations or time restrictions. [Observed]
- OGE receives copies of § 208 waivers and may comment on the grant, but cannot veto or revoke. [Observed — source: 5 C.F.R. § 2640.301]
- Waiver disclosure varies: some administrations publish all waivers proactively; others release waivers only in response to FOIA requests, sometimes with redactions. [Observed]
- The appointee participates in matters covered by the waiver; compliance with waiver conditions is monitored by the DAEO, but monitoring rigor varies by agency. [Inferred]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** Agency heads have sole authority to grant § 208 waivers within their agencies; the determination of whether a financial interest is "substantial" is discretionary and effectively unreviewable. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** OGE review is advisory only; no external authority can block a waiver grant, creating a self-authorizing system where the appointee's own agency decides the appointee's conflicts are acceptable. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** None exist within the waiver process itself; external accountability depends entirely on public disclosure, FOIA access, and congressional oversight attention. [Inferred]

### Common failure modes

- Waivers are granted with broad language that authorizes participation in entire categories of matters rather than specific decisions, extending the authorized conflict beyond the narrow case-by-case analysis the statute contemplates. [Inferred]
- Ethics pledge waivers (executive order) bypass the statutory § 208 analysis entirely, using a different legal basis with even less structured review criteria. [Observed]
- Non-disclosure of waivers prevents public accountability: when waivers are not proactively published, conflicts remain hidden until a FOIA request (which may take months or years) or media investigation surfaces them. [Observed]
- Waiver condition compliance is monitored by the DAEO within the appointee's own agency, creating self-policing dynamics. [Inferred]
- The "not so substantial" standard is subjective enough to accommodate virtually any financial interest, making the waiver mechanism a de facto opt-out from the conflict-of-interest prohibition. [Inferred]

### What evidence would prove/disprove key claims

- Compile waiver grants by administration, agency, and appointee and analyze the financial interests involved to test whether waivers are used narrowly or broadly. [Observed]
- Compare proactive waiver disclosure rates across administrations to quantify transparency variation. [Observed]
- Track whether appointees who receive waivers participate in decisions that benefit their prior employers or financial interests at higher rates than non-waivered appointees. [Hypothesis]
- Analyze DAEO monitoring records (if obtainable via FOIA) to test whether waiver condition compliance is actually tracked. [Hypothesis]
- Compare agency head waiver grant rates to OGE advisory opinions on the same waivers to measure whether OGE concerns affect grant decisions. [Inferred]
