# Presidential Memorandum vs Executive Order

**Summary:** Presidential memoranda and executive orders are both directives issued by the president to manage executive branch operations, but they differ in publication requirements, legal status, and political visibility. This matters because the choice between instruments affects transparency, legal durability, and congressional oversight capacity. Executive orders must be published in the Federal Register and numbered sequentially, creating a public record subject to tracking and review. Presidential memoranda carry equivalent legal force but face weaker publication norms, allowing presidents to issue binding directives with reduced public scrutiny. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** Presidential memoranda carry the same legal force as executive orders but face weaker publication and tracking requirements, enabling directive issuance with reduced transparency. [Observed]

### Actors and roles

- The president selects the directive instrument based on political visibility preferences, legal strategy, and institutional precedent for the policy area. [Observed]
- White House counsel advises on instrument selection, drafts directive text, and coordinates review with the Office of Management and Budget and affected agencies. [Observed]
- The Office of the Federal Register receives, reviews, and publishes executive orders with sequential numbering; memoranda publication depends on presidential direction. [Observed]
- Affected agencies implement both instruments identically, as legal force does not depend on the instrument label used by the president. [Observed]
- Congressional committees track executive orders through the Federal Register system but face greater difficulty monitoring presidential memoranda due to inconsistent publication and indexing. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- The president decides to issue a directive and White House counsel determines whether to use an executive order or presidential memorandum based on precedent, visibility, and legal strategy. [Observed]
- For executive orders, the draft is reviewed by OMB and OLC, signed by the president, filed with the Office of the Federal Register, and published with a sequential number. [Observed]
- For presidential memoranda, the draft follows similar internal review but publication in the Federal Register is discretionary unless the memorandum has general applicability and legal effect. [Observed]
- Agencies receive and implement the directive regardless of instrument type, as both carry equivalent legal authority under the president's Article II powers. [Observed]
- Subsequent administrations may revoke either instrument, though executive orders are more easily identified for revocation due to sequential numbering and systematic indexing. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** White House counsel controls instrument selection, determining whether a directive receives the transparency protections associated with executive order publication. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** The Office of the Federal Register is the sole publication channel for executive orders but has limited authority to compel publication of presidential memoranda. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** Courts review both instruments under the same legal standards, but unpublished memoranda are harder for litigants to identify and challenge. [Inferred]

### Common failure modes

- Strategic use of memoranda to avoid executive order tracking inflates apparent restraint in executive order counts while maintaining equivalent directive output. [Observed]
- Unpublished memoranda create accountability gaps because Congress, courts, and the public may not know binding directives exist. [Inferred]
- Instrument switching between administrations (reclassifying a memorandum as an order or vice versa) creates confusion about which directives remain in force. [Observed]
- Lack of a comprehensive presidential memoranda index makes historical research and compliance verification difficult for agencies and oversight bodies. [Observed]

### What evidence would prove/disprove key claims

- Compare executive order counts with presidential memoranda counts across administrations to test whether memoranda substitute for orders in maintaining directive output. [Observed]
- Track publication rates for presidential memoranda to measure transparency compliance across administrations. [Observed]
- Analyze subject-matter patterns to determine whether sensitive policy areas are disproportionately addressed through memoranda rather than orders. [Inferred]
- Evaluate whether a unified presidential directive registry would improve accountability without constraining legitimate flexibility in instrument selection. [Hypothesis]
