# Strategic Interagency Policy Coordination

Purpose: Explains how the NSC's statutory foundation gives national security interagency coordination structural advantages over domestic policy coordination — which relies on executive-order-created White House councils (NEC, DPC) without statutory authority — and how this asymmetry produces recurring domestic policy coordination failures.

Status: draft

Tags: interagency coordination, NSC, NEC, DPC, OMB, domestic policy, COVID-19, infrastructure permitting, accountability

Key actors:
- National Security Council (NSC) — statutory
- National Economic Council (NEC) — executive order, no statute
- Domestic Policy Council (DPC) — executive order, no statute
- Office of Science and Technology Policy (OSTP) — statutory
- Office of Management and Budget (OMB) — statutory, most powerful domestic coordinator
- Agency heads retaining independent statutory authority

Statutes and authorities:
- National Security Act of 1947, as amended, 50 U.S.C. § 3021 et seq. (NSC)
- National Science and Technology Policy, Organization, and Priorities Act of 1976, 42 U.S.C. § 6611 (OSTP)
- Executive Order 12835 (NEC, 1993)
- Executive Order 12859 (DPC, 1993)
- Reorganization Plan No. 2 of 1970 (OMB statutory basis)
- Administrative Procedure Act, 5 U.S.C. §§ 551–559

Case studies:
- COVID-19 pandemic response coordination failure (2020) — overlapping HHS, FEMA, DOD, CDC mandates
- NSC pandemic preparedness directorate abolished 2018 and not reconstituted
- Infrastructure permitting chronic coordination failure (multiple administrations)

Data sources:
