# Digital Surveillance and Civil Liberties Enforcement

Purpose: Explains how the FISA court's ex parte, classified review process for surveillance authorizations creates a structural accountability gap in which the legal basis for mass surveillance programs is determined in proceedings where only the government appears, and how this shapes civil liberties enforcement capacity.

Status: draft

Tags: surveillance, FISA, civil liberties, Fourth Amendment, intelligence, accountability, national security

Key actors:
- National Security Agency (NSA)
- Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI)
- Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court (FISC)
- Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board (PCLOB)
- Department of Justice (FISA Unit)
- Electronic Frontier Foundation, ACLU (civil society challengers)

Statutes and authorities:
- Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978, 50 U.S.C. § 1801 et seq.
- FISA Section 702 (50 U.S.C. § 1881a) — reauthorized 2024
- USA FREEDOM Act of 2015, Pub. L. 114-23
- Executive Order 12333 (bulk collection authority)
- Fourth Amendment, U.S. Constitution

Case studies:
- NSA bulk telephony metadata collection (Snowden disclosures, 2013)
- PCLOB Section 702 sunset review (2023–2024)
- *Clapper v. Amnesty International* (2013) — standing doctrine surveillance shield

Data sources:

- NSA transparency reports
