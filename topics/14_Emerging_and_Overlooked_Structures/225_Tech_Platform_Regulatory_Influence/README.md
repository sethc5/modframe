# Tech Platform Regulatory Influence

Purpose: Explains how major technology platforms deploy lobbying, technical complexity framing, and bipartisan political division to delay or weaken federal regulation, and how Section 230 liability immunity has become the central legislative battleground for platform governance.

Status: draft

Tags: tech platforms, lobbying, Section 230, antitrust, regulatory capture, FTC, DOJ, technology

Key actors:
- Meta, Google/Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, Microsoft — major platform operators
- NetChoice, Computer & Communications Industry Association (CCIA) — platform trade associations
- Federal Trade Commission (FTC) — antitrust and consumer protection
- Department of Justice Antitrust Division
- Senate Commerce Committee and House Energy and Commerce Committee
- European Commission (Digital Markets Act enforcement)

Statutes and authorities:
- Communications Decency Act, § 230, 47 U.S.C. § 230 (platform liability immunity)
- Sherman Act, 15 U.S.C. §§ 1–7, Clayton Act, 15 U.S.C. §§ 12–27 (antitrust)
- FTC Act, § 5, 15 U.S.C. § 45 (unfair or deceptive practices)
- EU Digital Markets Act (DMA), Regulation (EU) 2022/1925
- American Innovation and Choice Online Act — proposed, not enacted

Case studies:
- *United States v. Google LLC* (2024) — search monopoly ruling
- FTC v. Meta (Facebook) antitrust litigation (2021–present)
- KOSA (Kids Online Safety Act) legislative history
- EU DMA enforcement vs. Apple, Meta, Google (2024)

- GAO reports on digital platforms and regulatory gaps
- PolitiFact/NewsGuard platform content moderation data
