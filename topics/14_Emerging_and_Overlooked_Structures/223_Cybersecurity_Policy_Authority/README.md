# Cybersecurity Policy Authority

Purpose: Explains how federal cybersecurity authority is fragmented across CISA, NSA, CYBERCOM, FBI, and sector-specific regulators, with no single statutory authority to compel private sector compliance, and how this fragmentation produces coordination failures in major incident response.

Status: draft

Tags: cybersecurity, CISA, NSA, critical infrastructure, incident response, fragmentation, technology

Key actors:
- Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA, DHS)
- National Security Agency (NSA) / U.S. Cyber Command (CYBERCOM)
- Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI)
- Office of the National Cyber Director (ONCD)
- Sector-specific regulators (NERC, FCC, TSA, FDA)
- Private sector critical infrastructure owners and operators

Statutes and authorities:
- Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency Act of 2018, Pub. L. 115-278
- Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act (CIRCIA) of 2022, Pub. L. 117-103
- National Cybersecurity Strategy (2023, executive)
- National Cyber Incident Response Plan (NCIRP, 2016)
- Vulnerabilities Equities Process (VEP, executive order basis)

Case studies:
- SolarWinds supply chain attack (2020) — detection, attribution, response coordination gaps
- Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack (2021) — CISA/FBI coordination and private sector reporting
- Microsoft Exchange zero-day exploitation (2021) — VEP and disclosure timeline

Data sources:
- CISA advisories and known exploited vulnerabilities catalog
- ONCD National Cybersecurity Strategy implementation reports
- GAO cybersecurity high-risk list entries
- FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) annual reports
