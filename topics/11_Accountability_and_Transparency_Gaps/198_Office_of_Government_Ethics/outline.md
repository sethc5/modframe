# Office of Government Ethics (OGE)

**Summary:** The Office of Government Ethics was established by the Ethics in Government Act of 1978 to provide leadership and oversight of the executive branch ethics program, but its statutory design makes it a standard-setting and advisory body rather than an enforcement agency — OGE can issue rules, review financial disclosures, and recommend corrective action, but it cannot compel agencies to comply with its recommendations, cannot independently investigate or discipline officials, and depends on DOJ to pursue criminal referrals. [Observed] OGE's director is appointed by the President for a five-year term, providing some independence from electoral cycles, but the office's effectiveness is contingent on the administration's willingness to cooperate with ethics oversight. [Observed] The structural result is an ethics oversight body that functions effectively when executive branch leadership values ethics compliance but lacks the institutional tools to enforce compliance against a resistant administration or agency head. [Inferred] OGE's approximately 70 staff members set standards for roughly 4 million executive branch employees, creating a supervision ratio that depends entirely on agency-level implementation through the DAEO system. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** OGE sets government-wide ethics standards and reviews financial disclosures but depends on agency cooperation and DOJ prosecution to give its guidance force, making it an advisory body with regulatory authority but without enforcement power. [Inferred]

### Actors and roles

- **OGE Director** — appointed by the President, confirmed by the Senate, serves a five-year term; leads the office and sets ethics policy priorities. [Observed — source: 5 U.S.C. App. § 401] Incentive: maintain the integrity and credibility of the ethics program. Constraint: removal by the President; office budget subject to presidential budget request and congressional appropriation. [Observed]
- **OGE program staff** — approximately 70 employees who review financial disclosures, issue guidance, conduct agency ethics program reviews, and manage government-wide ethics data systems. [Observed] Constraint: small staff relative to the scope of the executive branch ethics program. [Observed]
- **Designated Agency Ethics Officials (DAEOs)** — serve as OGE's implementation arm within each agency; implement ethics programs, screen financial disclosures, and report to OGE on compliance. [Observed] Constraint: report to agency heads, not to OGE, creating dual-loyalty dynamics when agency leadership is the subject of ethics concerns. [Observed]
- **Agency heads** — receive OGE program review findings and recommendations; determine whether to implement them. [Observed] Constraint: no statutory obligation to accept OGE recommendations. [Observed]
- **DOJ Public Integrity Section** — receives criminal referrals from OGE; exercises prosecutorial discretion on whether to investigate and charge. [Observed]
- **Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee** — exercises oversight jurisdiction over OGE; confirms the OGE Director. [Observed]
- **The President** — appoints the OGE Director and shapes the administration's ethics culture; executive orders on ethics pledges can expand or contract OGE's operational scope. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- OGE issues government-wide ethics regulations (5 C.F.R. Parts 2634–2641) that establish standards of conduct, financial disclosure requirements, and conflict-of-interest procedures for executive branch employees. [Observed]
- DAEOs implement these standards within their agencies, screening financial disclosures, counseling employees, and managing ethics agreements (recusals, divestitures, waivers). [Observed]
- OGE conducts periodic agency ethics program reviews, evaluating DAEO performance, disclosure compliance, and ethics training completion. [Observed]
- OGE reviews financial disclosure reports for approximately 1,000 Senate-confirmed presidential appointees, identifying potential conflicts before confirmation. [Observed]
- When OGE identifies potential violations, it can: (a) recommend corrective action to the agency head, (b) refer the matter to DOJ for criminal investigation, or (c) issue a public letter documenting the concern. [Observed]
- Agency heads determine whether to implement OGE recommendations; OGE has no mechanism to compel compliance beyond public disclosure of its findings. [Observed]
- OGE publishes annual agency ethics program questionnaire data and maintains the executive branch financial disclosure database. [Observed]
- In extreme cases, OGE can issue a formal recommendation to the agency head — if the agency head does not comply within a reasonable period, OGE can notify the President; this authority has rarely been exercised. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** Agency heads control whether OGE recommendations are implemented; DOJ controls whether OGE criminal referrals are prosecuted; the President controls OGE Director appointment and removal. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** OGE's pre-confirmation financial disclosure review is its highest-leverage function — the only point where OGE's findings can directly affect whether an individual takes office. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** No entity can override an agency head's decision to reject OGE recommendations; OGE's escalation path (to the President) places the ultimate enforcement decision with the political authority most likely to share the agency head's perspective. [Inferred]

### Common failure modes

- OGE program reviews identify systemic ethics compliance problems but agencies implement corrective actions inconsistently, and OGE lacks follow-up enforcement mechanisms. [Observed]
- During administrations that are hostile to ethics oversight, OGE's advisory model becomes structurally ineffective because the office depends on good-faith engagement from the entities it oversees. [Inferred]
- The DAEO system creates a structural dependence on agency-level personnel who report to agency heads rather than to OGE, meaning OGE's standards are only as effective as the DAEO's willingness and ability to enforce them against their own leadership. [Observed]
- OGE's small staff cannot conduct comprehensive financial disclosure review for the full executive branch; it focuses on presidential appointees and relies on agency-level review for the vast majority of filers. [Observed]
- The five-year director term provides some insulation from political pressure, but it does not prevent a hostile administration from undermining OGE through budget cuts, non-cooperation, or slow-walking regulatory processes. [Inferred]
