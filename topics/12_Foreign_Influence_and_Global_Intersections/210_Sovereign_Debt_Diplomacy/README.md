# Sovereign Debt Diplomacy

Purpose: Explains how U.S. Treasury's veto-weight IMF shareholding, Paris Club creditor coordination role, and bilateral debt diplomacy translate U.S. financial power into structural influence over IMF program conditionality and sovereign debt restructuring terms, and how Chinese creditor competition has complicated this architecture.

Status: draft

Tags: IMF, sovereign debt, Treasury, CFIUS, Paris Club, G20 Common Framework, conditionality, China, financial diplomacy

Key actors:
- U.S. Treasury (International Affairs division, U.S. IMF Executive Director)
- International Monetary Fund (IMF board, staff, DSA methodology)
- World Bank Group (U.S. dominant shareholder)
- Paris Club (official bilateral creditor coordination)
- G20 Common Framework (post-2020 mechanism including China)
- China (PBOC, Exim Bank, China Development Bank) — competing creditor

Statutes and authorities:
- IMF Articles of Agreement, Article XII (voting structure, U.S. 17.4% share, 85% supermajority requirement)
- Bretton Woods Agreements Act, 22 U.S.C. § 286 et seq. (U.S. IMF membership basis)
- IMF and World Bank quota reform legislation (requires congressional action for U.S. share changes)

Case studies:
- Zambia sovereign debt crisis (2020-2023) — G20 Common Framework delays due to China-Paris Club creditor standoff
- Ghana debt restructuring (2024) — Common Framework completion after extended delay
- Greece bailout (2010-2012) — IMF conditionality in high-income-country crisis

Data sources:
- IMF lending decisions and program conditionality (imf.org, program archive)
- IMF quota and voting share tables
- Paris Club restructuring agreement records (clubdeparis.org)
- OECD bilateral creditor lending database
- IMF Independent Evaluation Office reports on conditionality
