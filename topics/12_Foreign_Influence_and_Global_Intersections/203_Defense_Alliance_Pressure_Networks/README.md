# Defense Alliance Pressure Networks

Purpose: Explains how allied governments exercise influence over U.S. defense posture, arms procurement, and basing decisions through multiple simultaneous channels — FARA-registered agents, defense industry domestic subsidiaries, embassy diplomatic contacts — while receiving substantially less disclosure scrutiny than equivalent adversary influence operations.

Status: draft

Tags: allied influence, FARA, defense industry, lobbying, NATO, diplomacy, export controls, ITAR

Key actors:
- NATO member state embassies and defense attachés
- Allied defense industry U.S. subsidiaries (BAE Systems Inc., Airbus US, Leonardo DRS, Rheinmetall US)
- FARA-registered allied-government agents
- Senate Armed Services Committee and House Armed Services Committee
- State Department and NSC
- CFIUS (for allied investment in U.S. defense firms)

Statutes and authorities:
- Foreign Agents Registration Act, 22 U.S.C. § 611 et seq.
- International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR), 22 C.F.R. Parts 120-130
- Export Administration Regulations (EAR), 15 C.F.R. Parts 730-774
- Lobbying Disclosure Act, 2 U.S.C. § 1601 et seq. (domestic subsidiary lobbying)

Case studies:
- Israel multi-channel U.S. Middle East policy influence (embassy, AIPAC, registered FARA agents, defense contracts)
- UK BAE Systems Inc. U.S. defense procurement relationships
- Saudi Arabia lobbying expansion post-2015 (FARA registrations)
