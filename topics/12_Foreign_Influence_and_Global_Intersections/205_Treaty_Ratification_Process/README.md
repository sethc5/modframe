# Treaty Ratification Process

Purpose: Explains how the constitutional two-thirds Senate ratification requirement has driven systematic executive branch substitution of executive agreements for formal treaties, concentrating international commitment authority in the executive branch and producing a posture where major U.S. international commitments (climate, nuclear, trade) are reversible by presidential action alone.

Status: draft

Tags: treaty ratification, executive agreements, Senate, Article II, foreign policy durability, Case-Zablocki, international law

Key actors:
- President and Secretary of State (treaty/executive agreement classification decision)
- Senate Foreign Relations Committee
- Full Senate (two-thirds ratification vote)
- State Department Office of the Legal Adviser (Circular 175 procedure)
- Congressional Research Service
- International negotiating partners (credibility concerns)

Statutes and authorities:
- U.S. Constitution, Art. II, § 2, cl. 2 (treaty clause)
- Case-Zablocki Act of 1972, 1 U.S.C. § 112b (executive agreement 60-day notification)
- State Department Circular 175 Procedure (treaty vs. executive agreement classification standard)

Case studies:
- Paris Agreement lifecycle: executive agreement entry (Obama 2016) → withdrawal (Trump 2017) → re-entry (Biden 2021) → withdrawal (Trump 2025)
- UNCLOS: submitted to Senate 1994, never ratified, pending on Senate calendar
- CTBT: submitted 1997, rejected by Senate 1999
- JCPOA (Iran nuclear deal): executive agreement, withdrawn January 2018, re-entered and then abandoned again
