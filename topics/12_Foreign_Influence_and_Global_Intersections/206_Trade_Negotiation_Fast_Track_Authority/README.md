# Trade Negotiation Fast Track Authority

Purpose: Explains how Trade Promotion Authority (TPA) delegates congressional tariff power to the executive branch in exchange for expedited, unamendable legislative consideration of completed trade agreements, and what the 2021 expiration of TPA means for U.S. trade negotiating capacity.

Status: draft

Tags: TPA, fast track, trade, USTR, congressional delegation, ITAC, tariffs, FTA, constitutional design

Key actors:
- Office of the United States Trade Representative (USTR)
- Senate Finance Committee and House Ways and Means Committee
- Full Congress (TPA implementing legislation vote)
- USTR Industry Trade Advisory Committees (ITACs)
- International FTA negotiating partners
- Labor and environmental advocates (TPA conditionality)

Statutes and authorities:
- Bipartisan Congressional Trade Priorities and Accountability Act of 2015 (TPA-2015), Pub. L. 114-26, 19 U.S.C. § 4201 et seq. (expired June 2021)
- Trade Act of 1974, 19 U.S.C. § 2101 et seq. (original fast-track authority)
- U.S. Constitution, Art. I, § 8, cl. 3 (Commerce Clause — tariff-setting power)

Case studies:
- USMCA negotiation and implementation (2018-2020) under TPA-2015
- TPA expiration (June 2021) and its effect on Indo-Pacific Economic Framework (IPEF) negotiating ambitions
- NAFTA implementing legislation (1993) as first major congressional-executive agreement under fast-track

Data sources:

- Congressional Research Service TPA analysis (RL33743)
- Peterson Institute for International Economics trade agreement outcome research
- WTO trade policy review mechanisms for U.S. trade commitments
