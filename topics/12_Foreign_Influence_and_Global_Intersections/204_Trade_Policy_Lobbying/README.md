# Trade Policy Lobbying

Purpose: Explains how concentrated producer interests systematically outperform diffuse consumer interests in trade policy through USTR industry advisory committee capture, formal AD/CVD petition processes, and informal USTR access, and how Section 301 product exclusion lobbying demonstrated the pattern at scale.

Status: draft

Tags: trade policy, USTR, lobbying, ITAC, antidumping, Section 301, TPA, WTO, producer interests

Key actors:
- Office of the United States Trade Representative (USTR)
- Department of Commerce International Trade Administration
- U.S. International Trade Commission (USITC)
- USTR Industry Trade Advisory Committees (ITACs)
- U.S. Chamber of Commerce, PhRMA, NAM, American Iron and Steel Institute
- Senate Finance Committee and House Ways and Means Committee

Statutes and authorities:
- Trade Act of 1974, 19 U.S.C. § 2101 et seq. (Section 201 safeguards, Section 301)
- Trade Agreements Act of 1979, 19 U.S.C. § 2501 et seq. (AD/CVD procedures)
- Bipartisan Congressional Trade Priorities and Accountability Act of 2015 (TPA), Pub. L. 114-26
- Tariff Act of 1930, 19 U.S.C. § 1673 et seq. (antidumping statute)

Case studies:
- Section 232 steel and aluminum tariffs (2018) — Commerce investigation to presidential proclamation to product exclusion lobbying
- Section 301 China tariffs (2018-2019) and product exclusion process
- ITAC advisory committee composition in USMCA negotiations

Data sources:
- USTR ITAC member rosters and ITAC annual reports
- USITC antidumping and countervailing duty database
- Commerce Department product exclusion records (Section 301)
- LDA trade lobbying filings by sector (lda.senate.gov)
