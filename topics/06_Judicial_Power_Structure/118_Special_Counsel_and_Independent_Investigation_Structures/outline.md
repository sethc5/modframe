# Special Counsel and Independent Investigation Structures

**Summary:** Federal law provides for the appointment of special counsels to investigate matters where the Attorney General faces conflicts of interest, creating an independent investigative structure that operates within the Department of Justice but with enhanced autonomy. The current special-counsel regulations (28 CFR Part 600) replaced the Independent Counsel Act after it expired in 1999, shifting appointment authority from a judicial panel to the Attorney General while granting day-to-day investigative independence. This structure creates inherent tension between investigative independence and political accountability, as the appointing AG retains removal authority and the special counsel depends on DOJ resources and institutional cooperation. [Observed]

**Mechanism in one sentence:** Special counsel regulations create semi-independent federal investigations within DOJ, balancing investigative autonomy against Attorney General oversight and removal authority. [Observed]

### Actors and roles

- The Attorney General determines when a conflict of interest warrants special counsel appointment and selects the appointee, retaining authority to remove the counsel for good cause. [Observed]
- Special counsels exercise investigative and prosecutorial authority within defined jurisdictional mandates, with day-to-day independence from DOJ supervision. [Observed]
- The Deputy Attorney General often exercises appointment authority when the AG is recused, creating an additional layer of political dynamics in the appointment decision. [Observed]
- Congressional oversight committees receive limited reporting on special counsel activities and exercise subpoena authority over investigation-related documents and testimony. [Observed]
- Target subjects of special counsel investigations challenge investigative scope, subpoena authority, and constitutional legitimacy through motions to quash and separation-of-powers arguments. [Observed]

### Process map (bulleted)

- The AG determines that a criminal investigation involves a conflict of interest or other extraordinary circumstance warranting outside appointment under the special counsel regulations. [Observed]
- The AG selects a special counsel from outside the government, defines the jurisdictional scope of the investigation, and provides DOJ resources and institutional support. [Observed]
- The special counsel conducts investigation and prosecution with day-to-day independence, reporting to the AG on major prosecutorial decisions and budget requirements. [Observed]
- The AG may countermand specific special counsel actions only after finding them "so inappropriate or unwarranted" as to require intervention, with notification to Congress. [Observed]
- Upon conclusion, the special counsel submits a confidential report to the AG, who decides what to disclose publicly, creating a final discretionary chokepoint. [Observed]

### Where power concentrates

- **Gatekeepers:** The Attorney General controls the threshold decisions—appointment, scope definition, and final-report disclosure—that frame the investigation's existence and public narrative. [Observed]
- **Bottlenecks:** DOJ resource allocation and institutional cooperation create operational bottlenecks, as the special counsel depends on department infrastructure, FBI support, and staff detail. [Observed]
- **Veto points:** The AG's removal authority for "good cause" and power to countermand specific actions function as veto points that could constrain investigative independence. [Observed]

### Common failure modes

- Political pressure on the AG to terminate, constrain, or influence the special counsel undermines the independence the regulations are designed to protect. [Observed]
- Scope limitations defined at appointment can exclude relevant conduct, while scope expansions trigger political controversy about investigatory overreach. [Observed]
- The AG's control over final-report disclosure creates opportunities for selective or misleading public characterization of investigation conclusions. [Observed]
- Constitutional challenges to special counsel authority—including Appointments Clause and unitary-executive arguments—create legal uncertainty that may delay or derail investigations. [Observed]
